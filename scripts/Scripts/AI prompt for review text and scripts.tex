Several data limitations should be kept in mind when interpreting the results.
\paragraph{Annual data frequency}
The empirical analysis relies on annual balance sheet data, which limits the 
ability to identify short-term adjustment dynamics. Banks may adjust their 
portfolios within a year in response to CCyB changes, and such within-year 
adjustments would not be captured, potentially attenuating the estimated 
effects. This concern is particularly relevant for the 2022 reactivation, 
where the eight-month transition period between the January 2022 
announcement and the September 2022 compliance deadline may have induced 
anticipatory within-year adjustments that annual data cannot distinguish from post-treatment responses.
Furthermore, since the outcome variables are computed as year-on-year growth rates, the first available observation is 2019 rather than 2018. This leaves no observable pre-treatment growth rate for the 2020 release, and only two pre-treatment observations 
for the 2022 reactivation. This prevents a formal event study test of the parallel trends assumption, which would typically require at least three to 
four pre-treatment periods, and should be kept in mind when interpreting the results  \parencite{lechner_estimation_2011}.
\paragraph{Credit risk data limitations}
Due to the confidentiality of supervisory data, the analysis does not have access to detailed mortgage characteristics such as LTV or LTI distributions. 
\textcite{behncke_effects_2023} uses these micro data to assess whether banks adjust the risk composition of newly issued mortgages in response to CCyB changes. The present analysis focuses instead on NPL ratios as an indirect measure of credit risk. However, NPL ratios are a lagging indicator that reflects lending decisions made several years prior, with credit risk typically emerging only gradually over time \parencite{jimenez_macroprudential_2017}. 
Given the relatively short observation period following each policy change, any shifts in lending standards within the mortgage portfolio induced by CCyB adjustments would be unlikely to show up in NPL ratios within the sample period. This limitation should be kept in mind when interpreting the credit risk results.
\paragraph{Concurrent policy measures}
A potential threat to identification arises from concurrent policy measures implemented during the COVID-19 crisis, including the leverage ratio relief, the COVID-19 credit guarantee program, and the SNB refinancing facility, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:covid measures}. The leverage ratio relief and SNB refinancing facility applied uniformly across all banks and are therefore largely absorbed by the year fixed effects $\gamma_t$. The COVID-19 
credit guarantee program targeted corporate loans rather than residential mortgages, suggesting that banks' participation may not be systematically correlated with their CCyB exposure. Finally, the sectoral CCyB was the only capital measure that varied systematically with banks' residential 
mortgage exposures. Nevertheless, I cannot rule out that these concurrent measures differentially affected high- versus low-exposure banks in ways not fully captured by the fixed effects. This remains a limitation of the analysis.
\paragraph{Banking sector structural break}
A structural break occurs in 2023 due to the emergency merger of CS into UBS, which is unrelated to CCyB policy. As UBS absorbed CS's entire loan portfolio, this event mechanically inflates UBS's mortgage growth in 2023--2024 and may affect estimates for the post-reactivation period.
